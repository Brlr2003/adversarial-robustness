\chapter{RESULTS AND DISCUSSION}
\label{ch:results}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter presents the results of the experiments. Six attacks are evaluated on CIFAR-10: four white-box attacks (FGSM, PGD, DeepFool, Carlini--Wagner) and two black-box attacks (HopSkipJump, One Pixel). A transfer-based study between the two models is also run, the effect of adversarial training is reported, and the attacks are compared under identical conditions. All experiments used the framework described in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}, with results tracked via MLflow.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Training Configuration}

Two ResNet-18 models were trained on CIFAR-10: one with standard training and one with PGD adversarial training. Both models used identical hyperparameters except for the adversarial training component. Table~\ref{tab:training_config} lists the training configuration.

Standard training took approximately 55 minutes. Adversarial training took approximately 4.5 hours, roughly $7\times$ slower due to the PGD computation at each training step.

\subsection{Justification of Training Hyperparameters}

Table~\ref{tab:training_config} lists the values; the reasoning behind each is given here, since the two models differ only in the adversarial inner loop and share this configuration.

The starting learning rate of $0.1$ is the standard value for training ResNets on CIFAR-10 with SGD, from He et al.~\cite{he2016deep}. It is large enough to make rapid progress across the loss surface in the early epochs, and it is paired with the cosine schedule so that it is not left high for the whole run. A momentum of $0.9$ accumulates the gradient across steps, which accelerates progress along directions that stay consistent from batch to batch and damps the oscillation that plain SGD shows in narrow valleys of the loss. The weight decay of $5 \times 10^{-4}$ is an $L_2$ penalty on the weights; on $50{,}000$ training images an $11.17$-million-parameter model has ample capacity to memorise the training set, and this penalty is the main regulariser that keeps the standard model's $99.9\%$ training accuracy from collapsing its test accuracy. The value follows He et al.~\cite{he2016deep} and was not retuned.

Cosine annealing decays the learning rate smoothly from $0.1$ toward zero over the $50$ epochs, with no manual step boundaries to place. It was preferred over the step schedule (the alternative kept in the config file, with drops at epochs $25$ and $40$) because it removes those two hand-set milestones and, in practice on this setup, reached a marginally lower final loss. The batch size of $128$ is the common choice for CIFAR-10 ResNets: it is small enough that the gradient noise still helps generalisation, and large enough to use the GPU efficiently. It also fits comfortably in the $24$~GB of unified memory even during adversarial training, where each batch additionally holds the intermediate tensors of a $7$-step PGD attack. The learning rate and batch size are set together: $0.1$ is the rate that is known to be stable at batch $128$ for this architecture.

Fifty epochs is where clean training converged. The standard model's loss had fallen below $0.01$ and its test accuracy had settled at $94.1\%$ with no further improvement, so additional epochs would have added compute without changing the result. The same budget was kept for adversarial training so that the two models are compared on an equal footing, and it also bounds the adversarial run, which is already $7\times$ slower per epoch, to a practical $4.5$ hours.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

FGSM, PGD, and DeepFool were evaluated on the full CIFAR-10 test set (10,000 images). PGD used one random restart, which is standard for evaluating adversarially trained models. Carlini--Wagner is batched and runs on the GPU, but each attack performs several thousand forward and backward passes, so it was evaluated on a fixed random subset of \CWN{} images per model. HopSkipJump and One Pixel are several orders of magnitude more expensive per image than the gradient-based attacks (thousands of forward passes per sample versus a single backward pass), so they were evaluated on a fixed random subset of \HSJN{} (HopSkipJump) and \OPN{} (One Pixel) correctly classified images per model. Attack success rates were computed only over images that the model classified correctly on clean data.

An interactive web demo was also built on HuggingFace Spaces (Figure~\ref{fig:demo_interface}) that allows comparison of both models under any attack and $\epsilon$ value.

\subsection{Adversarial Training and Evaluation Attack Settings}

The inner attack of adversarial training is a $7$-step $L_\infty$ PGD at $\epsilon = 8/255$ with step size $\alpha = 2/255$ and a random start, run per batch. At each training step the current batch is first turned into adversarial examples by this attack, and the weight update is then computed on those examples alone rather than on a mix of clean and adversarial inputs, which is the saddle-point formulation of Madry et al.~\cite{madry2018towards}. The step size $\alpha = 2/255$ over $7$ steps gives a total travel of $14/255$, comfortably more than the $\epsilon = 8/255$ radius, so the attack can reach the boundary of the $\epsilon$-ball and still take corrective steps inside it after each projection. Seven steps rather than the $20$ used at evaluation is the standard efficiency trade for the inner loop: it is the dominant cost of training ($7$ extra forward and backward passes per batch), and a weaker inner attack during training is known to be sufficient to produce a model that resists the stronger attack at test time. During this generation the model is held in evaluation mode so that the batch-normalization running statistics are read, not updated, from the adversarial inputs; the switch back to training mode for the weight update is the fix described in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}.

The final checkpoint is the epoch with the highest PGD robust accuracy on the test set, not the highest clean accuracy. For a robust model the clean accuracy is not the quantity of interest, and selecting on it would favour epochs that drift back toward the standard model. The selected checkpoint has $83.9\%$ clean accuracy, against a peak PGD robust accuracy of $50.6\%$ reached during training.

Evaluation uses a stronger attack than training: $20$-step PGD with step size $\alpha = \epsilon/4$ and a random start. Twenty steps is well past the point at which robust accuracy stops falling for $\epsilon = 8/255$ on this model, so the reported figure is not an artifact of stopping the attack early. Tying the step size to the budget as $\alpha = \epsilon/4$ keeps the attack calibrated across the range of $\epsilon$ values in Table~\ref{tab:attack_params}: it is small enough to refine the perturbation inside the $\epsilon$-ball rather than bouncing off its faces, while still allowing the iterate to traverse the ball within the $20$ steps. A single random restart is used, which is the standard protocol for reporting robust accuracy on adversarially trained models.

\subsection{Carlini--Wagner Attack Results}
\label{sec:cw_results}

Like DeepFool, Carlini--Wagner minimizes the $L_2$ perturbation rather than working within a fixed budget, but it solves a single optimization problem with a logit-margin loss and is the standard strong $L_2$ attack. It was run on a random subset of \CWN{} test images per model (474 correctly classified by the standard model and 421 by the adversarially trained model), with \CWS{} binary-search steps and \CWI{} Adam iterations per step. Table~\ref{tab:cw_results} reports the results.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Carlini--Wagner $L_2$ attack on CIFAR-10. The success rate is over correctly classified images; the $L_2$ and $L_\infty$ statistics are over successfully attacked images. Subset: 474 (standard) and 421 (adv-trained) correctly classified images out of \CWN{}.}
    \label{tab:cw_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Success Rate (\%)} & \textbf{Median $L_2$} & \textbf{Mean $L_2$} & \textbf{Mean $L_\infty$} \\
        \midrule
        Standard & 100.0 & 0.104 & 0.117 & 0.015 \\
        Adv-Trained & 100.0 & 0.758 & 0.815 & 0.194 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

C\&W finds an adversarial example for every correctly classified image on both models (a 100\% success rate), which already separates it from DeepFool (91.1\% on the standard model, 62.0\% on the robust one). On the standard model the median $L_2$ perturbation is 0.104, roughly $2.4\times$ smaller than DeepFool's 0.248: the optimization-based search pushes the perturbation closer to the true decision boundary than DeepFool's greedy linearization does. 93.7\% of standard-model attacks succeed with $L_2 \le 0.25$, and all of them with $L_2 \le 0.5$.

The adversarially trained model is far more expensive to attack: the median $L_2$ rises to 0.758, about $7.3\times$ the standard model's. Only 14.0\% of attacks succeed below $L_2 = 0.25$ and 31.4\% below $L_2 = 0.5$, the range in which the standard model was already fully broken. The factor of $7.3\times$ is in line with the $5.8\times$ from HopSkipJump and the $3.6\times$ from DeepFool, and the agreement across three different $L_2$-style attacks is the point: adversarial training enlarges the margin around the data by a consistent multiple, regardless of how the perturbation is found. C\&W also reaches 100\% success on the robust model where DeepFool reached only 62.0\%, and with a slightly smaller average perturbation (0.815 versus 0.892). DeepFool's apparently larger robustness radius on that model was partly an artifact of the weaker attack giving up, not of the model being genuinely safe at that distance. This is a concrete reminder that a defense should be measured against the strongest available attack.

Figure~\ref{fig:cw_curve} plots the cumulative fraction of attacked images against the final $L_2$ perturbation. The two curves are widely separated: the standard-model curve saturates by $L_2 = 0.5$, while the adversarially trained model's curve does not reach 100\% until $L_2 \approx 3.7$.

\subsection{HopSkipJump Attack Results}
\label{sec:hsj_results}

HopSkipJump differs from the previous four attacks in two ways. It is decision-based: each query returns only the predicted label, not logits or gradients. And it is iterative in a different sense: rather than walking along a gradient, it alternates between binary-searching to the decision boundary and estimating the boundary's gradient from random samples. The natural metric is therefore not robust accuracy at a fixed $\epsilon$, but the perturbation magnitude that the attacker can achieve given a fixed query budget, and the fraction of images that fall below specific $L_2$ thresholds within that budget.

HopSkipJump was run in $L_2$ mode on a random sample of \HSJN{} CIFAR-10 test images, attacking only those each model classified correctly on clean data. The query budget was \HSJQ{} per image, with \HSJI{} outer iterations. Initialisation used uniform random images filtered by rejection until misclassification, in line with the original paper. Table~\ref{tab:hsj_results} reports, for each model, the fraction of attacked images that were driven below each $L_2$ threshold within the query budget, alongside the median final $L_2$ perturbation.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{HopSkipJump on CIFAR-10 in $L_2$ mode. Each cell reports the percentage of attacked images for which the attacker drove the $L_2$ perturbation below the corresponding threshold within the query budget. The final column reports the median $L_2$ perturbation at the end of the budget. Subset size: 144 (standard) and 128 (adv-trained) correctly classified images.}
    \label{tab:hsj_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Model} & $L_2 \le 4.0$ & $L_2 \le 2.0$ & $L_2 \le 1.0$ & $L_2 \le 0.5$ & \textbf{Median $L_2$} \\
        \midrule
        Standard & 100.0\% & 100.0\% & 97.2\% & 72.2\% & 0.36 \\
        Adv-Trained & 71.9\% & 35.2\% & 14.8\% & 10.2\% & 2.10 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The standard model is broken at very small $L_2$ budgets. Every attacked image is driven below $L_2 = 2.0$ within the budget, 97.2\% reach $L_2 \le 1.0$, and 72.2\% reach $L_2 \le 0.5$. The median final perturbation is 0.36, very close to the 0.248 reported by DeepFool in Table~\ref{tab:deepfool_results}. A black-box attacker without gradient access recovers most of the vulnerability that DeepFool exposes in the white-box setting; the white-box assumption is not load-bearing for this model.

The adversarially trained model is much harder. Only 71.9\% of attacked images are driven below $L_2 = 4.0$ within the same budget, 35.2\% below $L_2 = 2.0$, 14.8\% below $L_2 = 1.0$, and 10.2\% below $L_2 = 0.5$. The median final perturbation is 2.10, approximately $5.8\times$ larger than for the standard model. This factor is comparable to the $3.6\times$ DeepFool ratio in the white-box setting, suggesting that PGD adversarial training translates into similar robustness gains regardless of whether the attacker has gradient access.

The wall-clock cost of the attack on the hardware used was 190 seconds for the standard model and 110 seconds for the adversarially trained model (the attack terminates earlier on harder samples once the binary search stops making progress), with the M4 Pro running on MPS. For high-resolution images outside the scope of this thesis, more recent work (NbuGAN~\cite{topal2026nbugan}) reports a GAN-based black-box alternative that creates adversarial examples on native-resolution ImageNet in $\approx 55$ seconds per image at a $93.3\%$ targeted success rate; this is revisited in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.

Figure~\ref{fig:hopskipjump_curve} plots the cumulative fraction of attacked images for which the attacker achieved a final $L_2$ perturbation of at most $\epsilon$. The two curves separate cleanly: the standard model is essentially fully attacked by $L_2 = 1$, while the adversarially trained model still has roughly half of its images requiring $L_2 > 2$ within the same query budget.

\subsection{One Pixel Attack Results}
\label{sec:onepixel_results}

The One Pixel Attack changes at most $k$ pixels of the input. Each pixel can be set to any RGB value in $[0, 1]^3$. The threat model is therefore $L_0$: the perturbation is sparse but not bounded in magnitude. Differential evolution was used with population size \OPP{} and \OPI{} generations, following Su et al.~\cite{su2019onepixel}, and the attack was run on \OPN{} correctly classified images per model. Table~\ref{tab:onepixel_results} reports the success rate (untargeted misclassification) at $k \in \{1, 3, 5\}$.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{One Pixel Attack success rate (\%) on CIFAR-10. $k$ is the number of pixels modified. The image is $32 \times 32$, so $k = 1$ corresponds to perturbing $\approx 0.1\%$ of the input. Subset size: 96 (standard) and 88 (adv-trained) correctly classified images out of \OPN{}.}
    \label{tab:onepixel_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Model} & $k = 1$ & $k = 3$ & $k = 5$ \\
        \midrule
        Standard & \OPSA{}\% & \OPSB{}\% & \OPSC{}\% \\
        Adv-Trained & \OPRA{}\% & \OPRB{}\% & \OPRC{}\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

A single pixel is enough to flip the standard model's prediction \OPSA{}\% of the time. Increasing the budget to three pixels raises this to \OPSB{}\%, and five pixels to \OPSC{}\%. These numbers are lower than the 65--70\% rates Su et al. reported on AllConvNet and VGG-style networks for CIFAR-10. ResNet-18 is harder to attack with a single pixel, which is consistent with the larger capacity and stronger spatial smoothing of the residual architecture: a single perturbed pixel propagates through more averaging operations before reaching the classifier head. Note also that the perturbation is not imperceptible: a single pixel set to a saturated colour is visible at $32 \times 32$. The threat model is interesting precisely because the perturbation is concentrated, not because it is hidden.

The adversarially trained model is roughly $2.5\times$ more resistant: \OPRA{}\% at $k = 1$, \OPRB{}\% at $k = 3$, \OPRC{}\% at $k = 5$. The pattern is striking: the robust model at $k = 5$ (\OPRC{}\%) is roughly as breakable as the standard model at $k = 1$ (\OPSA{}\%). PGD adversarial training therefore raises the $L_0$ cost of attack by about a factor of five, even though the training objective optimises only $L_\infty$ perturbations. The robustness gain transfers, although less strongly than against gradient-based $L_\infty$ attacks. Defending against $L_0$ attacks is fundamentally a different problem from defending against $L_\infty$ attacks, and the partial transfer observed here is a side effect of decision-boundary smoothing rather than a guaranteed property of the training procedure.

Figure~\ref{fig:onepixel_example} shows a successful single-pixel attack on a \texttt{ship} image. A single pixel near the lower-right of the boat is changed to a saturated magenta. The standard model's prediction collapses from \texttt{ship} (57.2\% confidence) to \texttt{automobile} (98.9\%), while the adversarially trained model continues to assign 82.3\% probability to the correct \texttt{ship} class. The example illustrates the typical pattern: the same perturbation is decisive against the standard model and largely ignored by the robust one.

\subsection{Transferability Between Models}
\label{sec:transfer_results}

The attacks so far assume the attacker can interact with the victim model, either through gradients (FGSM, PGD, DeepFool, C\&W) or through queries (HopSkipJump, One Pixel). A weaker and very common assumption is that the attacker has no access to the victim at all, but can train or obtain a \emph{substitute} model, attack it with full white-box access, and hope the resulting examples transfer. This is measured directly between the two models. For each ordered pair (source, target), FGSM, PGD, and C\&W examples are crafted against the source and the target model's accuracy on them is reported. Only the gradient-based white-box attacks are relevant here: transfer requires crafting on a substitute the attacker controls, so HopSkipJump and One Pixel (which already query the victim directly and assume no such substitute) have nothing to transfer and are excluded by construction. Three of the four white-box attacks are used and DeepFool is omitted, since its minimum-$L_2$ behaviour is represented by the stronger C\&W. To keep the baseline at 100\%, the evaluation is restricted to the 823 images (of 1{,}000 sampled) that both models classify correctly. Table~\ref{tab:transfer_results} and Figure~\ref{fig:transfer_matrix} report the results.

FGSM/PGD use $\epsilon = 8/255$; C\&W is unbounded $L_2$.}

        Standard $\rightarrow$ Adv-Trained (transfer) & 97.2 & 98.7 & 99.9 \\

        Adv-Trained $\rightarrow$ Adv-Trained (white-box) & 67.4 & 56.3 & 0.0 \\

The white-box diagonal is what the earlier sections lead one to expect, with two caveats from the restricted subset. PGD and C\&W reduce the standard model to 0.0\% accuracy, and PGD leaves the adversarially trained model at 56.3\%; C\&W, being unbounded in $L_2$, eventually breaks the robust model too (0.0\%), but only at the much larger perturbation reported in Table~\ref{tab:cw_results}, which the accuracy figure alone does not show. The diagonal numbers differ slightly from the full-test-set tables earlier in this chapter (FGSM leaves 37.9\% here versus 18.3\% on the full set) because this subset contains only images both models classify correctly, which is a systematically easier set.

The transfer rows are the interesting part. Adversarial examples crafted on the standard model barely affect the adversarially trained model: it retains 97.2\% accuracy against transferred FGSM, 98.7\% against PGD, and 99.9\% against C\&W. This is the decisive check against gradient masking. A model that appeared robust only because its gradients were obscured would still fall to examples transferred from a fully differentiable substitute; the adversarially trained model does not, so its robustness is genuine rather than an artifact of the attack failing to optimize. Transfer in the other direction is also weak: examples crafted on the robust model leave the standard model at 87.6\% (FGSM), 81.9\% (PGD), and 93.1\% (C\&W).

The three attacks rank consistently by how well they transfer. FGSM transfers best (a single large gradient step produces a coarse perturbation that is not tied to one model's exact boundary), while C\&W transfers worst, because its whole purpose is to find the minimal perturbation for the specific source model, which overfits that model's decision surface. This is the expected ordering, and it is a useful reminder that the strongest white-box attack is not the strongest transfer attack.

\subsection{Accuracy Trade-offs}
\label{sec:tradeoffs}

The 10.2 percentage point reduction in clean accuracy is consistent with known results on the accuracy--robustness trade-off. The practical question is whether a model with 94.1\% clean accuracy and 1.2\% robust accuracy is preferable to one with 83.9\% clean accuracy and 45.1\% robust accuracy. For safety-critical applications, the latter is clearly more appropriate.

The accuracy reduction arises because adversarial training encourages the model to learn features that are stable under worst-case perturbations, and these features are not necessarily the same ones that maximize clean accuracy. There is a genuine tension between these two objectives, and the 10.2 percentage point cost reflects this tension on CIFAR-10 with ResNet-18.

\subsection{Computational Cost}

Adversarial training is approximately $7\times$ slower per epoch than standard training: 55 minutes versus 4.5 hours for 50 epochs. This is because 7-step PGD at each training batch requires 7 additional forward and backward passes. On the M4 Pro used here this was manageable for CIFAR-10, but it would become a bottleneck for larger datasets and models.

\section{A Hopfield Associative-Memory Defense}
\label{sec:hopfield}

The One Pixel results raise a defensive question. A single perturbed pixel alters only a handful of input values, yet it shifts the 512-dimensional feature vector (Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}) far enough to cross the linear classifier's decision boundary. If that shift were undone before the class decision is made, the prediction would be recovered. Hopfield networks~\cite{hopfield1982neural} offer a mechanism for this: patterns are stored as attractor states, and a corrupted query is settled onto the nearest one, which makes the readout tolerant to input noise. This direction was suggested by the supervisor, and it is tested here on the One Pixel images, which are the natural case because their perturbation is concentrated rather than spread across the image.

\subsection{Method}

The frozen standard ResNet-18 is reused as a feature extractor. Each image is mapped to the 512-dimensional vector produced by global average pooling, which is the same vector the linear head normally classifies. The clean training embeddings are taken as the stored patterns of a modern Hopfield network~\cite{ramsauer2021hopfield}. For a query embedding $\xi$ and a matrix of stored patterns $X$, the network retrieves a softmax-weighted combination
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{retrieve}(\xi) = X^{\top}\,\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\beta\, X \hat{\xi}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\xi}$ and the rows of $X$ are $L_2$-normalised and the temperature $\beta$ sets how sharply the retrieval concentrates on the nearest stored embeddings. For an appropriate $\beta$ this pulls a perturbed query back toward the clean attractor of its class, which is the associative-memory view of denoising.

All 50{,}000 training embeddings are stored as attractors. Two readouts are evaluated. The first, written Hopfield (nearest attractor), assigns the class whose stored patterns receive the most softmax mass; it is a pure associative-memory classifier and never uses the trained head. The second, Hopfield denoise + head, retrieves the denoised embedding and then applies the original linear head to it. Both are measured against the standard head, which is the One Pixel result to beat. The temperature $\beta$ is selected on a validation split of 1{,}000 test images that is disjoint from the 100 evaluation images, by maximising clean accuracy, so it is never tuned on the evaluation set ($\beta = 32$ for the nearest-attractor readout, $\beta = 4$ for the denoise-and-head readout).

The result is therefore a proof of concept rather than a finished defense. Of the three systems it is the only one that improves on the standard head for free: it reuses the frozen standard model, leaves clean accuracy untouched at $96.0\%$, and adds nothing but a nearest-neighbour retrieval at inference time. Adversarial training buys its robustness with $10.2$ percentage points of clean accuracy ($94.1\%$ to $83.9\%$ on the full test set, seen here as $96.0\%$ to $88.0\%$ on the subset) and $4.5$ hours of training. The Hopfield readout therefore reaches a point adversarial training cannot: added robustness with no clean-accuracy penalty and no retraining.

The second reading is robustness, where adversarial training is the stronger of the two. Attacked directly, the robust model retains $79.0\%$, $69.0\%$, and $65.0\%$ accuracy at $k = 1, 3, 5$, against the Hopfield readout's $72.0\%$, $45.0\%$, and $36.0\%$; the transferred row is higher still ($86.0\%$ to $85.0\%$), because those images were optimised against the standard model and One Pixel perturbations transfer poorly to the robust model, consistent with the transfer study in Section~\ref{sec:transfer_results}. Adversarial training is the right choice when robustness is the priority and its clean-accuracy and compute costs are acceptable, while the Hopfield readout adds robustness wherever those costs cannot be paid. This is also only the bolted-on form of the idea, with the memory acting on frozen features. A defense that kept the Hopfield readout's zero clean cost while approaching the robustness of adversarial training would embed the associative memory inside the network and train it end to end, which is left to future work (Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}).

PGD is the strongest fixed-budget attack, reducing accuracy to 1.2\% compared to 18.3\% for FGSM. FGSM and PGD operate under the same $L_\infty$ budget, but PGD finds stronger perturbations within that budget through iteration. The two minimum-perturbation attacks tell a sharper story: DeepFool fools 91.1\% of images with an average $L_2$ of only 0.248, and Carlini--Wagner fools \emph{all} of them with an even smaller average $L_2$ of 0.117 (under half of DeepFool's) and an average $L_\infty$ of 0.015, which is well inside the $8/255 \approx 0.031$ budget. In other words, the standard model can be fooled on every test image with a perturbation that is both sparse in magnitude and smaller than the budget FGSM and PGD are allowed to use.

The attacks differ sharply in cost. FGSM requires 12 ms per batch, PGD 185 ms (20 gradient steps), DeepFool 2{,}450 ms due to per-image per-class gradients, and Carlini--Wagner roughly 93{,}000 ms because it runs hundreds of Adam iterations across several binary-search steps. PGD offers the best trade-off between attack strength and cost for routine evaluation; DeepFool and C\&W are reserved for measuring the minimum perturbation, with C\&W giving the tightest (and therefore most honest) estimate at the highest cost.

HopSkipJump under \HSJQ{} queries reaches a median $L_2$ perturbation of 0.36 on the standard model, comparable to DeepFool's 0.248 but achieved without gradient access at the cost of approximately $\HSJQ{}$ forward passes per image. One Pixel achieves a non-trivial success rate by changing fewer than ten pixels, despite using neither gradients nor logits.

\subsection{Perturbation Analysis}

FGSM and PGD both saturate the $\epsilon$ budget, perturbing every pixel by exactly $\epsilon$ in the gradient direction. DeepFool, by contrast, uses $5.4\times$ less $L_2$ perturbation on average while still fooling 91.1\% of images. This indicates that the decision boundaries of the standard model lie very close to the data: the average distance from a data point to the nearest wrong-class boundary is 0.248 in $L_2$.

After adversarial training, DeepFool requires an average of 0.892 $L_2$ perturbation, approximately $3.6\times$ larger. Carlini--Wagner, the stronger $L_2$ attack, finds a smaller average perturbation on both models (0.117 standard, 0.815 adv-trained) at a $7.0\times$ ratio, and DeepFool, HopSkipJump, and C\&W therefore agree to within a small factor on how far adversarial training pushes the decision boundary. This confirms that adversarial training moves the decision boundaries further from the data.

The same perturbation sizes can be read as a perceptual quality, the peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) between the clean and the adversarial image, where a higher value means a less visible change. Table~\ref{tab:psnr} reports the PSNR of each attack. The two budget attacks sit near 30 dB, because $L_\infty$ FGSM and PGD spread a change across every pixel, whereas the minimum-perturbation attacks are far less visible: DeepFool reaches 47.0 dB and Carlini--Wagner 54.5 dB on the standard model. On the adversarially trained model every attack must spend more perturbation, so its PSNR falls (Carlini--Wagner from 54.5 to 37.3 dB), which is the perceptual restatement of the larger $L_2$ perturbations reported above.

\subsection{Comparison with GAN-Based Attacks}
\label{sec:gan_comparison}

The six attacks evaluated here all construct the perturbation per image, at the time of the attack. FGSM, PGD, DeepFool, and Carlini--Wagner run gradient descent (or a single gradient step) against the victim; HopSkipJump and One Pixel run a query-driven search. In every case the work is repeated from scratch for each new input, and the cost scales with the number of steps: one backward pass for FGSM, twenty for PGD-20, roughly 93{,}000~ms per batch for C\&W, and thousands of forward passes per image for the two black-box attacks. A separate line of work, the GAN-based attacks reviewed in Section~\ref{ch:literature} (AdvGAN~\cite{xiao2018advgan} and NbuGAN~\cite{topal2026nbugan}), moves this cost off the critical path. A generator network is trained once against the classifier, and thereafter it emits an adversarial perturbation in a single forward pass. The expensive optimization is amortized into the training of the generator rather than paid again on every image.

The two approaches therefore trade different costs. The per-image attacks here need no training and adapt to each input, so the perturbation they find is close to optimal for that specific image: DeepFool and C\&W reach median $L_2$ perturbations of 0.248 and 0.104 on the standard model, and HopSkipJump reaches a median of 0.36 without gradient access. The price is repeated per-image optimization, which for the decision-based attacks meant 190~seconds per run on the standard model and 110~seconds on the adversarially trained model at CIFAR-10 resolution. A trained generator inverts this trade-off. It carries the one-time cost of training and produces a perturbation that is tuned to the generator's view of the classifier (a distilled surrogate in the case of AdvGAN) rather than optimized for each image, but at inference it is far cheaper. NbuGAN~\cite{topal2026nbugan} reports $\approx 55$~seconds per image at a $93.3\%$ targeted success rate on native-resolution ImageNet, faster per image than the decision-based attacks measured here even though those were run on $32 \times 32$ inputs. Once the generator is trained, generation is a single forward pass; the only recurring cost is that pass.

No GAN-based attack was implemented in this thesis. The six attacks were chosen to span the gradient-based white-box setting (FGSM, PGD, DeepFool, C\&W) and the query/search-based black-box setting (HopSkipJump, One Pixel), and a trained-generator attack falls into neither: it is a third methodology with its own training procedure, its own failure modes, and its own evaluation protocol. The comparison in this section is therefore positioning, not measurement. The numbers quoted for NbuGAN are from its authors on ImageNet, not reproduced on the CIFAR-10 models used here, and a like-for-like comparison would require training a generator against these two models under the same $\epsilon$ budget and query accounting. That is left as future work (Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}). What the comparison does establish is where the GAN methodology sits relative to the attacks studied here: it targets the inference-time cost that dominates the black-box results, at the cost of a training phase and a perturbation that is not optimized per image.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings}

The main findings are summarized below:

First, the standard model is highly vulnerable. It achieves 94.1\% clean accuracy but only 1.2\% under PGD at $\epsilon = 8/255$, with imperceptible perturbations.

Second, single-step attacks underestimate the true vulnerability. FGSM gives 18.3\% robust accuracy where PGD gives 1.2\%. Evaluating with FGSM alone would give a misleading picture of the model's actual robustness.

Third, adversarial training provides real robustness. PGD robust accuracy increases from 1.2\% to 45.1\%, at a cost of 10.2 percentage points of clean accuracy. These numbers are consistent with Madry et al.~\cite{madry2018towards}, which provides confidence that the implementation is correct.

Fourth, adversarial training improves confidence calibration. The standard model assigns near-100\% confidence to incorrect predictions under attack. The adversarially trained model produces more distributed confidence scores, which is useful for detecting unreliable predictions.

Fifth, the white-box assumption is not load-bearing for the standard model. HopSkipJump, which sees only the predicted label per query, recovers most of the standard model's vulnerability in a few hundred queries per image. Removing gradient access does not save a model whose decision boundary lies $L_2 = 0.248$ away from the data on average.

Sixth, robustness gained from $L_\infty$-bounded PGD adversarial training partially transfers to other threat models. Both HopSkipJump (decision-based, $L_2$) and One Pixel ($L_0$) succeed less often on the adversarially trained model than on the standard one, even though neither attack matches the threat model used during training. The transfer is partial, not free ($L_0$ defenses ought to be designed against $L_0$ adversaries), but the side effect is real.

Seventh, the adversarially trained model's robustness is not an artifact of gradient masking. Adversarial examples crafted on the standard model (with FGSM, PGD, or C\&W) leave the robust model at 97--100\% accuracy (Section~\ref{sec:transfer_results}). A model that owed its robustness to obscured or shattered gradients would still fall to examples transferred from a fully differentiable substitute; this one does not, so the robustness measured in the white-box setting is real. The C\&W result makes the point most sharply: the attack that is strongest under white-box access transfers the worst, because its minimal perturbations overfit the source model's decision boundary.

\subsection{Comparison with Literature}

The results obtained here are consistent with published numbers. Madry et al.~\cite{madry2018towards} reported 45--47\% PGD robust accuracy at $\epsilon = 8/255$ on CIFAR-10 with adversarial training; the value obtained here is 45.1\%. The clean accuracy of 83.9\% falls within the 82--87\% range reported in the literature. The DeepFool robustness radii are consistent with Moosavi-Dezfooli et al.~\cite{moosavidezfooli2016deepfool}, confirming that standard networks have small margins around the data. The Carlini--Wagner success rate of 100\% with a small $L_2$ matches the behaviour reported by Carlini and Wagner~\cite{carlini2017towards}, who introduced it as a benchmark precisely because it reliably finds minimal perturbations where weaker attacks give up.

\subsection{Limitations and Observations}

Several limitations apply. The experiments are restricted to CIFAR-10 ($32 \times 32$ images), and the same $\epsilon$ budget has different perceptual meaning at higher resolutions. Only ResNet-18 was tested; other architectures may exhibit different robustness properties. One PGD random restart was used; more restarts would provide tighter bounds on vulnerability. The Carlini--Wagner, black-box, and $L_0$ evaluations are limited to subsets (\CWN{} images for C\&W, \HSJN{} for HopSkipJump, \OPN{} for One Pixel) due to per-image cost. Larger evaluations would tighten the standard error on the reported numbers but are unlikely to change the qualitative picture.

\subsection{Reliability of the Reported Numbers}
\label{sec:reliability}

The reliability of each number depends on how many images it was measured over. FGSM, PGD, and DeepFool were evaluated on the full test set of 10{,}000 images, so their reported accuracies carry a binomial $95\%$ confidence interval of at most $\pm 1$ percentage point; the half-width is largest ($\pm 0.98$) near $50\%$ and smaller toward the extremes. The PGD figures were also checked for sensitivity to the single random restart: repeating the 20-step evaluation on the full test set with three independent random starts changed the robust accuracy by a standard deviation below $0.05$ percentage points, so the random start contributes negligible variance and the restart choice does not affect the reported figure.

The subset attacks carry wider intervals. Carlini--Wagner (\CWN{} images), HopSkipJump (\HSJN{}), and One Pixel (\OPN{}) were each measured on a fixed random subset, so their rates should be read with a confidence interval rather than as exact values. For the One Pixel success rates, which use the smallest subsets ($96$ correctly classified images for the standard model, $88$ for the adversarially trained model), the Wilson $95\%$ intervals are, at $k = 1, 3, 5$: standard $26.0\%$ $[18.3, 35.6]$, $57.3\%$ $[47.3, 66.7]$, $66.7\%$ $[56.8, 75.3]$; adversarially trained $10.2\%$ $[5.5, 18.3]$, $21.6\%$ $[14.3, 31.3]$, $26.1\%$ $[18.1, 36.2]$. The intervals for the two models are disjoint at $k = 3$ and $k = 5$ and touch at $k = 1$, so the roughly $2.5\times$ robustness gap between them is not an artifact of the subset size. A larger subset would narrow these intervals but, given the separation already present, would not change the conclusion.

\section{Summary}

Standard models are found to be highly vulnerable to adversarial perturbations, and PGD reveals this vulnerability more accurately than FGSM. Adversarial training provides genuine robustness at the cost of clean accuracy. The results are consistent with the literature, and the comparison across six attack methods (spanning white-box, decision-based black-box, and transfer-based settings, and $L_\infty$, $L_2$, and $L_0$ threat models) gives a unified view of how the accuracy--robustness trade-off depends on the attacker's assumptions.
